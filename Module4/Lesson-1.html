Introduction to “A Short Introduction to the Two Row Wampum”:

Historic Treaties were signed by the British or Canadian Crown between 1701 and 1923. (As you will learn, the Two Row Wampum (1613) occurred prior to this time and was inherited from a period of colonization by the Dutch.) Modern-day Treaties are also referred to as “current” because they were signed after 1975.

Both the Anishinaabe and Haudenosaunee Peoples created wampum belts that symbolized treaty agreements among and between Indigenous Nations. As various European nations arrived on Turtle Island, Indigenous Nations made similar agreements with them. The Two Row Wampum is an agreement between the Dutch and the Haudenosaunee that has become a widely acknowledged example of non-interference between European nations and Indigenous Nations. This article provides insight into how these agreements were viewed by Indigenous Peoples, as assurances that the parties involved would not pass customs, or laws, or engage in activities that would impede the other’s right to self-determination as Peoples.

A Treaty is an agreement between two sovereign nations; in Canada that most often means between the Canadian government (the Crown) and First Nations that are recognized in the Canadian constitution.
